IDM241 Case Study

Overview

For this microinteractions class, I used HTML, CSS, and Javascript to recreate and make improvements to microinteractions. The component I decided to follow were the product cards on Behance.

Through an iterative three-phase process (Alpha, Beta, and Final), I determined all the triggers, rules, feedback, loops and mode of each, and then built the microinteractions according to my descriptions.

Context and Challenges

  1. Background

From the end of September until the first week of December, I have been the solo UX/UI designer and front-end and back-end developer of this course-long microinteractions project.

  1. Goals/Objectives

All changes and additions I made to the microinteractions I created for the product card had to be improvements and useful to the component. To ensure this, while I was creating the descriptions I thought about the functionality the microinteraction would serve and in what ways would that improve a users experience if at all.

Process and Insights:

This project was split into 3 phases: Alpha, Beta, and Final. In order to ensure quality work and follow the timeline in an orderly fashion, the Alpha was designed to help me lay the groundwork and figure out a starting point to creating microinteractions.

  1. Alpha

I started by visiting a couple websites trying to gain an understanding of different components and their microinteractions. From there, I tried to think of potential improvements

After that, I played around with the colors to decide how I wanted the first impression to be. I ultimately decided on a blue theme based on my preferences. I wanted to try something new as I tend to lean towards a pinker color palette and was hoping to give myself a challenge. In addition, the fonts I had chosen were further a welcoming feeling with the bubble letters inviting whimsy and cute charm.

Aside from the aesthetics, I also focused on laying the foundational technical work. This includes data-mining and cleaning. After receiving the folder of files I had to come through, I was able to organize it into a table separating important valuables with one another which will be more important later in Beta. Despite not needing the database to be linked at this time, it was vital for me to start ASAP so that when Beta is assigned, I can get started immediately.

  1. Beta

After Alpha, I ran the colors I originally used through a color contrast checker and realized that the blues I had chosen could be difficult to read. Because my main goal was to make gathering these recipes more accessible, I ended up changing into a higher contrast color palette I was more familiar with: pink and magenta (put in hex codes)

After determining the color palette, the base I laid in Alpha allowed me to build my PHP and run it through rounds of trial and error. The pinks I chose seemed to be more friendly to the eye as well as more interesting to look at. I finally got to lay out all of the data I had collected and successfully inputted into a table.

At this point, the database of the recipes I was collecting earlier has now been implemented to the Beta project so I know how each of the recipes will look like on the design I have made, this also allowed me to ensure the site's responsiveness and debug anything that may have been lost when connecting my data.

  1. Finals

Based on the critiques from the last projects, I knew that I had to design at a bit of a higher level, include more images, and ensure all of my fonts were loading. At this point, my design is set in stone, it was a matter of making sure that the PHP code was clean.

Troubleshooting why some images loaded in while some did not, investigating why fonts were not always loading, and also making sure I kept my code consistent is where I had lacked. To do this, I started implementing a higher level of microinteractions into the project. I have added hover states where I can to push the narrative I want to portray of something fun and whimsical. In addition, I went back to make sure all the images were loading by combing through typos. Lastly, ensuring that the font loads is a rookie mistake, but going through each line of your CSS and making sure it is as optimized as possible takes a lot of time but I managed to get it done.

Solution

With these improvements, it leaves us with my final recipe project:


You may not be able to tell by this screen but behind this has gone through many internal conflicts of design hierarchy, spending lots of efforts to make sure my database connection was clean, and rounds of critiques. Because of all of that, I was able to make "Recipes" what it is at its current stage. A recipe website that is easily navigable and organized geared for people who want to have more fun.

Defining features that would determine whether or not I was successful would be the search bar, a wide range of filters to sift through, and easily readable text. For this to be as efficient and as fun as I wanted it to be I tackled these core things.

  1. Efficiency

Coming from the stack of pdfs we first received, the product I went on to design was to give a place where all of those recipes could live at once. To prevent fatigue from the tiring search it would've been to read through all those recipes, efficient design allows users to search for ingredients they want to use with ease. This is solved with my implementation of both the search and filter keys.

  1. First Impressions

Cooking can be intimidating, especially when you might be given a large stack of recipes you've never seen before. To ease users in, I designed a landing screen to invoke fun and add some character which is not common in most other recipe websites (based on personal competitor analysis).Using bright and bold/bubbly aesthetics brings a warmth to the brand and invites more people to come!

Results

Because this project is a reflection of a hypothetical recipe site, its success can be hard to determine. Despite it all, the overall design is successful in achieving its goal to be efficient and fun. If given more time, I would have liked to include more microinteractions to make the website definitely pop. I would also try to steer away from my signature color to really push myself to design for a wider palette.
